## 1. Tarikh Authorship and Manuscript Tradition

### 1.1 Title and Scope

Full title: **Ta'rikh al-Islam Dhibat/Diba Mahall** (تاريخ اسلام ديبا محل)
English: "The Islamic History of the Maldive Islands"
Language: Arabic
Coverage: From the Islamic conversion (548 AH / 1153 CE) through approximately 1822 CE
Duration covered: approximately 670 years of Maldivian history

### 1.2 Authors (Three Generations of the Qadi Family)

**Primary author: Qadi Hasan Taj al-Din (حسن تاج الدين)**
- Office: Chief Justice (Fandiyaru) of the Maldives
- Died: 27 Shawwal 1139 AH / 27 February 1727 CE
- Political context: Served under the Dhiyamigili dynasty sultans
- His portion covers: conversion (1153) through approximately the early 18th century
- Bias: As a court qadi, his account reflects the perspective of the Islamic scholarly establishment; he situates the Maldives within universal Islamic history stretching back to the Rashidun Caliphs
- Method: Drew on earlier Loamaafanu (copper plate grants), oral court traditions, and possibly the Raadhavalhi

**First continuation: Muhammad Muhibb al-Din (محمد محب الدين)**
- Relationship: Nephew of Hasan Taj al-Din
- Born: 1118 AH / 1706 CE
- Died: 1199 AH / 1784-1785 CE
- Office: Chief Justice (Sheikhul Islam)
- Political context: Lived through the Dhiyamigili-to-Huraagey transition, the Malabar invasion of 1752, and the subsequent expulsion; deeply involved in court factions
- Continuation covers: Early-to-mid 18th century events

**Second continuation: Ibrahim Siraj al-Din (ابراهيم سراج الدين)**
- Relationship: Grandson of Muhibb al-Din (per Peacock 2020); also described as grandson of Hasan Taj al-Din through the female line (per royalark.net Dhiyamigili genealogy)
- Died: After 1243 AH / after 1827 CE
- Office: Chief Justice, multiple terms (1787-1788, 1789-1792, 1806)
- Full name per royalark.net: Qazi Ibrahim Siraj ud-din Fandiyaru Manikufa'anu
- Born: 1750 at Male
- Mother: Princess Fatima Rani Kilege (b. September 1722), daughter of Sultan Ibrahim Iskandar II (P78)
- Father: Ahmad Muhi ud-din Khatib Manikufa'anu (killed February 1753)
- Died: 18 February 1831 at Male
- Political context: Witnessed the consolidation of Huraagey rule
- Continuation covers: Late 18th to early 19th century (through approximately 1822 CE)

### 1.3 Manuscript Tradition and Editions

- **TUFS Edition (critical edition):** Edited by Hikoichi Yajima (家島彦一)
  - Volume 1 (1982): Arabic text facsimile, 133.7 MB PDF (DOI: 10.15026/92528)
  - Volume 2 (1984): Annotations and Indices, 204 pages, 3 PDF files (DOI: 10.15026/84064)

  - Series: Studia Culturae Islamicae (SCI-22)
  - ISBN: 4872975057

- **Bell copy (1920s):** H.C.P. Bell received a copy of the Tarikh from King Mohamed Shamsuddine III (P91) during his archaeological surveys

- **Manuscript provenance:** The original manuscripts were held within the qadi family and the Maldivian court. The TUFS edition is based on manuscript facsimiles obtained through Japanese-Maldivian academic cooperation.

### 1.4 Political Biases (per Peacock 2020)

Peacock's key argument (Asiatische Studien 74(1): 195-220, 2020) identifies three intertwined agendas:

1. **Pious/Ethical Agenda:** The Tarikh uses historical narratives to impart moral lessons to Maldivian sultans. Good rulers are praised for piety; bad rulers are condemned for impiety.

2. **Islamic Universalist Agenda:** The work situates the Maldives within the grand narrative of Islamic history, beginning with the Rashidun Caliphs. This is unusual for a chronicle of a small island polity and reflects the qadi class's cosmopolitan orientation.

3. **Factional Political Agenda:** The authors were not neutral chroniclers but active participants in Maldivian court politics. Their personal experiences and factional affiliations shaped their presentation of events. Hasan Taj al-Din served under the Dhiyamigili dynasty, while his successors lived through the turbulent transitions of the 18th century. The Tarikh is thus simultaneously a historical chronicle and a political document.

Peacock describes the Tarikh as "a major but unexploited source" for both Maldivian and broader Indian Ocean history.

### 1.5 Relationship to Other Sources

| Source | Language | Overlap | Key Differences |
|--------|----------|---------|-----------------|
| Raadhavalhi | Dhivehi | Shares source material (oral traditions, Loamaafanu) | More locally focused; Dhivehi historiographic framework |
| Ibn Battuta Rihla | Arabic | Overlaps on 1340s events (Khadijah, Ahmad) | Eyewitness; contradicts on conversion narrative |
| Loamaafanu copper plates | Dhivehi (Eveyla/Dhives Akuru) | Tarikh draws on these | Primary epigraphic evidence; independently datable |

- The Tarikh notably records three female sultanas in the Lunar dynasty (Khadijah, Raadhafathi/Fatima, Dhaain), which is unusual in Islamic historiography

- The Hilaaly dynasty was marked by extreme political instability: frequent depositions, restorations, and assassinations
- Kalu Mohamed (#51) had three separate accessions -- unprecedented in the Tarikh
- The Portuguese period (1558-1573) represents the only non-Islamic interregnum; Dom Manoel converted to Christianity and was baptized by St. Francis Xavier
- The Tarikh records the Chinese embassy to Sultan Yusuf II in 1421 (Zheng He period)

- The child sultan Kuda Mohamed (P70) was killed along with his mother in a palace coup
- Mohamed Mohyedine (P71) introduced a strict penal code (fundamentalist orientation)

### 3.4 The Hilaaly-to-Huraagey Bridge

This is one of the most genealogically complex and politically significant lineage claims in the Tarikh tradition:

1. Dom Manoel / Hassan IX (P61, Hilaaly) married Dona Leonor de Ataide (Portuguese)
2. Their son Dom Joao (P66) married Dona Francisca de Vasconcelos (P101)
3. Their daughter Infanta Dona Ines de Malvidas (P213) married Captain Sebastiao Tavares de Souza
4. Their son Dom Luis de Souza (P97) became titular king of the Maldives
5. Dom Luis died without legitimate issue (1656), but the Huraagey founder Hassan Izzuddine (P80) is described as descending from this line

This bridge connects the Hilaaly dynasty (Islamic/Portuguese) to the Huraagey dynasty (fully Islamic again) through a Christian-era intermediate generation. The Tarikh presents this as legitimate dynastic continuity.

## 6. Conversion Narrative

### 6.1 The Tarikh Account (Tabrizi Tradition)

The Tarikh credits the conversion of the Maldives to **Yusuf Shams al-Din al-Tabrizi** (يوسف شمس الدين التبريزي), a Persian scholar from Tabriz. According to this tradition:

- The last Buddhist king Dhovemi (P2) was converted in 548 AH / 1153 CE
- The converter was a holy man who demonstrated the power of Islam by banishing the sea demon Rannamaari through Quranic recitation
- Dhovemi adopted the Muslim title Sultan Muhammad al-Adil (The Just)
- The Tabrizi is locally honored as "Tabrizugefanu"
- His tomb is venerated at the Hukuru Miskiy in Male

### 6.2 The Ibn Battuta Account (Barbari/Berber Tradition)

Ibn Battuta (visiting 1343-44) credits the conversion to **Abu al-Barakat Yusuf al-Barbari** (أبو البركات يوسف البربري), described as a Berber/Moroccan from the Maghreb.

### 6.3 The Arabic Script Ambiguity

The key scholarly insight (noted by multiple scholars) is that in the Arabic manuscript tradition, the words **al-Barbari** (البربري) and **al-Tabrizi** (التبريزي) are very similar when written without full diacritical marks (i'jam). In early Arabic script, several consonants were undifferentiated:

- ب (ba), ت (ta), ث (tha), ن (nun), ي (ya) all shared the same base form
- Without dots: "ٮوسڡ الٮٮرٮرى" could be read as either "Yusuf at-Tabrizi" or "Yusuf al-Barbari"

This paleographic ambiguity means the two traditions may refer to the **same individual** whose nisba (geographic epithet) was misread by different copyists.

### 6.4 The Somali Hypothesis

A third scholarly position identifies Abu al-Barakat with **Yusuf bin Ahmad al-Kawneyn** (Sheikh Aw-Barkhadle), a Somali Muslim scholar and alleged founder of the Walashma dynasty in the Horn of Africa. This identification is based on:
- The term "al-Barbari" in medieval Arabic could refer to the Somali coast (the "Barbaroi" of Greek geography = modern Berbera)
- Chronological overlap with al-Kawneyn's traditional dates
- Ibn Battuta may have been recording a Somali origin, not a Moroccan one

### 6.5 Evidence of Pre-Conversion Islamic Contact

- Cairo Geniza letter (1141 CE, 12 years before conversion) already mentions Maldivian cowrie shells in Arabic trade
- Arab geographers (al-Masudi, al-Idrisi, Abu Zayd al-Sirafi) describe the Maldives from the 9th-12th centuries
- The conversion was likely gradual through trade contacts, not a sudden event caused by one individual

### 6.6 Evidence of Violent Conversion

The Tarikh and associated sources record that the conversion was not entirely peaceful:
- The Isdhoo Loamaafanu (1195 CE) documents Sultan Dhinei (P5) ordering the destruction of the Buddhist monastery on Isdhoo
- Monks were forcibly converted and those who refused were executed
- Buddhist stupas and statues were systematically destroyed

## 7. Contradictions with Other Sources

### 7.1 Tarikh vs. Ibn Battuta

| Topic | Tarikh | Ibn Battuta | Assessment |
|-------|--------|-------------|------------|
| Converter identity | Yusuf Shams al-Din al-Tabrizi (Persian) | Abu al-Barakat Yusuf al-Barbari (Berber) | Unresolved; may be same person with misread nisba |
| Khadijah's grandfather | Not specifically named as Bengali | "Sultan Salahuddin Salih al-Bangali" | Ibn Battuta's claim of Bengali ancestry is unique and unconfirmed |
| Khadijah's reign | Presented within annalistic framework | Eyewitness detail of court life | Complementary; Ibn Battuta provides richer social context |
| Date of conversion | 548 AH / 1153 CE | Consistent | Agreement |
| Number of Khadijah's reigns | Three | Witnessed one | Consistent (Ibn Battuta was present during first reign) |

### 7.2 Tarikh vs. Raadhavalhi

| Topic | Tarikh | Raadhavalhi | Assessment |
|-------|--------|-------------|------------|
| Converter | Tabrizi | Also Tabrizi | Agreement (both local traditions) |
| Framework | Universal Islamic history (Rashidun onward) | Locally focused Dhivehi chronicle | Different historiographic traditions |
| Language | Arabic | Dhivehi | Different intended audiences |
| Source material | Loamaafanu, oral traditions | Same base sources | Both derivative of same core tradition |

### 7.3 Tarikh vs. Loamaafanu Copper Plates

The royalark.net genealogy notes: "The tarikh (1692 AD) seems to be at variance with the Isdhoo lomafanu (1197 AD)" regarding succession details. The copper plates, being contemporary primary sources, should generally be preferred over the Tarikh (which was composed 500+ years after the events it describes for the early period).

### 7.4 Tarikh vs. European Sources

| Topic | Tarikh | European Sources | Assessment |
|-------|--------|-----------------|------------|
| Portuguese period | Framed as apostasy and occupation | Framed as evangelization and alliance | Opposing perspectives |
| Dom Manoel conversion | Condemned | Celebrated (by Portuguese) | Ideological opposition |
| Guillaume Adam (1316) | n/a | "Islands had recently converted to Islam, which is still not strongly established" | Suggests conversion was still partial 160+ years after official date |
| Pyrard de Laval (1602-07) | n/a | Detailed court descriptions | Complements Tarikh for the Portuguese-Utheemu transition period |

### 7.5 Madhab (Legal School) Contradiction

- Ibn Battuta (1343-44) explicitly states the Maldives followed the **Shafi'i** madhab
- The factsanddetails.com synthesis claims the **Maliki** school was adopted
- The Tarikh tradition generally supports Shafi'i orientation (consistent with South Asian and Indian Ocean Islamic networks)
- Modern scholarly consensus: The Maldives has historically been **Shafi'i**, consistent with Ibn Battuta and the broader Indian Ocean pattern

### 9.3 Known Gaps

1. **Full Arabic text extraction:** The TUFS facsimile (133.7 MB PDF) was identified but not page-by-page read in this extraction round. Direct Arabic text extraction from the facsimile would yield additional names, dates, and details not captured by secondary scholarship.

2. **Peacock 2020 full article:** The article's full text was behind a paywall (De Gruyter) and the St Andrews repository PDF could not be rendered through web fetch tools. The abstract and metadata were extracted but the detailed genealogical analysis within the article (pages 195-220) remains incompletely captured. Future extraction should obtain the PDF through institutional access.

3. **Peacock 2018 Sufi Cosmopolitanism chapter:** This chapter (pp. 53-80 in "Challenging Cosmopolitanism") was identified as discussing lineage and royal power in the Maldives but the full text was access-restricted. It specifically discusses the case of Muhammad Shams al-Din (P72, the Hamawi sultan) and the role of Sufi scholars in enforcing sharia-minded piety.

4. **TUFS Annotations volume (Vol. 2):** Three PDFs totaling 63.6 MB were identified but not fully parsed. These annotations and indices likely contain transliterated name lists, chronological tables, and cross-references that would significantly enrich this extraction.

5. **Tombstone inscriptions at Hukuru Miskiy:** The coral stone cemetery contains dated Arabic inscriptions for past sultans. No systematic extraction exists.

6. **Intelligence in the Sultanate of Maldives (Taylor & Francis, 2024):** An article specifically interpreting the Tarikh was identified (DOI: 10.1080/18335330.2024.2431019) but was access-restricted. This could contain significant new analysis.
