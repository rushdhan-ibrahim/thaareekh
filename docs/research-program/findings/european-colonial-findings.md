## 1. H.C.P. Bell Findings

### 1.1 Published Works Catalog

H.C.P. Bell (Harry Charles Purvis Bell, 1851-1937) was a British/Ceylon civil servant and the first Commissioner of Archaeology in Ceylon (1890-1912). His Maldives corpus comprises:

| Work | Date | Publisher | Pages | Availability | Notes |
|------|------|-----------|-------|-------------|-------|
| **The Maldive Islands: An Account of the Physical Features, Climate, History, Inhabitants, Productions, and Trade** | 1883 | Frank Luker, Acting Government Printer, Colombo | iv + 133 (+ 5 foldouts) | Internet Archive (full text digitized): archive.org/details/maldiveislandsac00hcpb | His earliest Maldives publication; Section IV "Historical Sketch" contains dynastic material. Reprinted by Asian Educational Services, New Delhi, 2004 (ISBN 8120612221). |
| **Excerpta Maldiviana** | 1922-1926 (serialized) | Journal of the Ceylon Branch of the Royal Asiatic Society (JCBRAS) | Multiple installments | Reprinted by Asian Educational Services (ISBN 8120612213) and Gyan Publishing House (ISBN 8121233747) | Comprehensive catalog of Maldivian historical documents; includes translations from Divehi; Portuguese intercourse section; old photographs and ancient document reproductions. A subsection specifically covers "The Portuguese intercourse with the Maldivians." |
| **The Maldive Islands: Monograph on the History, Archaeology, and Epigraphy** | 1940 (posthumous) | Ceylon Government Printing Press, Colombo | ~204 pp | Rare; WorldCat OCLC 866581748; Google Books snippet (id: N3lEHQAACAAJ) | THE foundational European academic source. Published 3 years after Bell's death. Co-edited with W.L. De Silva. Covers history, archaeology across atolls, and epigraphy including inscription readings. |
| **Ceylon Sessional Papers (various)** | 1879-1922 | Ceylon Government | Various | Cambridge CSAS Bell Family Papers; some via DPLA | Archaeological survey reports from his visits starting in late 1879. Include atoll-by-atoll archaeological surveys. |

**Bell Family Papers** are held at the Centre of South Asian Studies, Cambridge University (2 boxes). Box 1 contains letters and diaries (1916-1937); Box 2 contains papers relating to the Archaeological Survey of Ceylon (report from 1899) and biographies of figures in Sri Lankan archaeology.

### 1.2 Genealogical Claims Extracted

Bell's 1883 work references the sultan system but the full genealogical material is in the 1940 monograph (not digitized online). From the 1883 text, Bell references Pyrard's observation that the sultan held the title "Sultan Ibrahim dolos assa ral tera atholon" ("Ibrahim Sultan Roy of thirteen provinces and of twelve mil isles").

Bell's key contribution to genealogical understanding includes:
- Named the Eveyla Akuru script (used on the Isdhoo Loamaafaanu copperplate inscription, dated 1194 CE / 590 AH), claiming it resembled medieval Sinhala script
- Documented coral stone inscriptions and tombstone inscriptions across multiple atolls
- Studied the ancient mounds called *havitta* or *ustubu* (from *chaitiya* and *stupa*), found on many atolls, proving pre-Islamic Buddhist civilization
- His king list in the 1940 monograph would be the most authoritative European academic sovereign sequence

**First visit to Maldives**: Late 1879, ordered there officially. Returned multiple times across several decades through early 20th century. The king of the Maldives provided his own royal schooner *Fath-ul-Majid* for Bell's archaeological surveys of southern atolls.

### 1.3 King List / Sovereign Table

Bell's 1940 monograph contains the authoritative European academic king list, but the text is not digitized online. The Excerpta Maldiviana (1922-1926) also contains chronological sovereign data drawn from Divehi documents Bell translated.

**Comparison with project data**: Cannot be completed without direct access to the 1940 monograph. The project's sovereign list (P1-P95) is primarily derived from Maldives Royal Family website (maldivesroyalfamily.com), which itself draws on Bell and the Raadhavalhi/Tarikh traditions. Bell's list would serve as Grade A/B corroboration for the entire chain.

### 1.4 Title/Office Descriptions

Bell documented the Eveyla Akuru script terms on copperplate grants, which include official titles and administrative language. His work on epigraphy established the reading of early Maldivian inscriptional terminology.

### 1.5 Inscriptional Evidence with Genealogical Content

**Isdhoo Loamaafaanu** (1194 CE / 590 AH): The oldest copperplate book discovered in the Maldives, written in Eveyla Akuru script. Bell named this script. The inscription dates to the reign of Siri Fennaadheettha Mahaa Radun (Dhinei Kalaminja) -- this corresponds to **P5** in the project data (Sultan Dhinei, regnal name Fennaadheeththa, reign 1193-1199). The Loamaafaanu records are official grants given by ruling kings to individual mosques, bestowing on them benefits from various islands.

**Coral stone and tombstone inscriptions**: Bell documented inscriptions across multiple atolls during his archaeological surveys, particularly in Addu and Fuvahmulah atolls. One havitta on Fuvahmulah was specifically noted.

### 1.6 Assessment of Bell's Reliability and Biases

**Strengths**: First-hand archaeological fieldwork; access to royal cooperation; systematic documentation; epigraphy expertise from his Ceylon work.

**Biases**: Bell was determined to prove pre-Islamic Buddhist civilization preceded Muslim conversion -- a thesis that colored his interpretive framework. His readings of inscriptions emphasized Sinhala/Buddhist continuities. His colonial-era perspective may have undervalued oral Maldivian traditions.

**Grade**: A/B (primary fieldwork + near-contemporary documentary access, but colonial-era interpretive biases must be noted).

## 2. Portuguese Period Findings

### 2.1 Dom Manoel / P61 Narrative (Detailed Chronology)

The RoyalArk genealogical database (royalark.net/Maldives/maldive5.htm) provides the most detailed European-sourced genealogical reconstruction of the Portuguese period. Key findings:

**Dom Manoel (Sultan Hassan IX / P61)**:
- Born: 1531 at Male (RoyalArk) vs. project data yb:1525
- Died: Before July 10, 1584 (RoyalArk) vs. project yd:1585
- Parents: Prince Ahmad Manikufa'anu Kalaminja and Golavehi Aisha Rani Kilege
- Usurped throne from his half-brother before May 2, 1549
- Installed as Sultan Hassan IX Svasi Sri Dhirukusalokya Maha Radun
- Fled to Cochin in 1551 (two years nine months into reign)
- **Converted to Catholicism: January 1, 1552** at Cochin under Portuguese protection
- Styled as His Highness Dom Manoel with expanded titles
- **Restored by Portuguese: May 19, 1558** but remained in Cochin/Goa
- Islands governed by Portuguese-appointed regents including Andiri Andirin
- First marriage: Dona Maria (Christian convert, died before 1553)
- Second marriage: Dona Leonor de Ataide (1554 at Goa), daughter of Dom Heitor de Sousa de Ataide

**CONTRADICTION WITH PROJECT DATA**: Project lists P61 birth as 1525, RoyalArk as 1531. Project lists death as 1585, RoyalArk as before July 10, 1584. Project lists reign periods [[1550,1552],[1558,1573],[1573,1583]], while RoyalArk gives usurpation before May 2, 1549 and death before 1584. The conversion date (January 1, 1552) is a key detail not in the project data.

- Born c.1560 at Cochin as Dom Joao de Malvidas

- Regnal title Sri Kithiri Maha Radun

### 2.4 Dom Philippe / P67 and Donna Ines

**Dom Felipe (P67)**:
- Born 1593 at Goa as Dom Felipe de Malvidas
- Proclaimed titular King following father's death, 1604
- Title: Sri Kathiri Maha Radun
- Received Knight of the Order of Christ, June 1618
- Died unmarried and without issue before 1639 at Goa
- **MAJOR CONTRADICTION**: Project data says P67 yb:1588, yd:1635. RoyalArk gives 1593-before 1639.
- **CRITICAL**: RoyalArk says Dom Felipe died "s.p. unm." (without issue, unmarried). Project shows him as having a son. The scroll.in article states Dom Philippe was killed in the 1632 expedition to Maldives. This contradicts RoyalArk which says he died before 1639 at Goa.

**Infanta Dona Inez de Malvidas**:
- Daughter of Dom Joao and Dona Francisca de Vasconcelos (i.e., sister of Dom Felipe)
- Married at Goa in 1610: Captain Sebastiao Tavares de Souza (Portuguese fidalgo, Captain of Canara Fort 1610-1612, Daman 1612)
- **CRITICAL for Huraagey connection**: She is the mother of Dom Luis de Souza

### 2.5 Dom Luis and the Huraagey Connection

**Dom Luis de Souza** (titular King of the Maldives c.1639-1656):
- Born 1612 at Goa
- Son of Captain Sebastiao Tavares de Souza and Infanta Dona Inez de Malvidas
- Succeeded his maternal uncle (Dom Felipe) as titular King c.1639
- Visited Portugal 1641; received by King Joao IV with title of Highness confirmed
- Joined Portuguese expedition to Maldives 1650; wounded during attack on Male
- Implicated in 1653 attempted overthrow of Portuguese Viceroy Count de Obidos
- Arrested and imprisoned at Mormugao until Nov 10, 1655
- Died unmarried aboard "Nossa S. de Graca" off Mozambique coast in 1656
- Named King of Portugal as heir to the Eleven Thousand Islands
- May have had natural son: Manuel de Sousa da Silva

**Brother**: Dom Manoel Malavis (de Malvidas?) -- joined 1650 Portuguese Maldives expedition; killed in attack on Male.

### 2.6 Hilaaly-to-Huraagey Genealogical Bridge (from MRF)

### 2.7 Portuguese Administrative Structure in Maldives

The claim of "Portuguese rule" is contested. Key points from maldivesroyalfamily.com analysis:
- Andiri Andirin was described as "Goa Kalu Faranji" (black Frank/Christian of Goa) -- his dark skin suggests non-European, possibly Indian-Christian origins
- He operated as a mercenary commander/regent, not a colonial administrator
- Portuguese chroniclers (Barros, Correia, Castanheda, Couto) referred to Maldives in context of regional power struggles, not as a colonial possession
- The "Portuguese rule" narrative was systematically constructed in the 20th century by Hussain Salahuddine, who replaced "Kaafaru/Nasorah" (infidel/Christian) with "Portuguese" in historical texts
- Mohamed Amin Dorhimeyna Kilegefan established National Day (Rabee-al-Awwal) in late 1940s to commemorate Thakurufan's victory

### 2.10 St. Francis Xavier and the Maldives Conversion

The claim in the project data that P61 was "baptised by St Francis Xavier" requires careful assessment. My extensive search found NO direct evidence that Francis Xavier personally visited the Maldives or baptized Hassan IX/Dom Manoel. Xavier was in Goa and south India during the early 1550s and died in December 1552 off the Chinese coast. The conversion occurred January 1, 1552 at Cochin. While Xavier was active in Portuguese India at this time, direct baptism by Xavier specifically is not confirmed in the search results. This claim should be downgraded to confidence grade C or D unless corroborated by a primary source.

### 3.2 Maldives-Focused Content (Volume 1)

Chapters V-VI: Shipwreck of the "Corbin" on Maldivian reefs and subsequent survival
Chapters X-XII: Descriptions of Maldivian geography, religion, and ceremonial practices
Chapters XIII-XVII: Local customs, government structure, social hierarchy, and economic systems
Chapters XVIII-XXI: Political events and maritime incidents
Chapter XXIII: Bengali expedition to Male and the Maldivian king's death

### 3.3 Offices/Titles Described

Pyrard provides the most detailed contemporary European account of the Maldivian court structure during the early Utheemu period (c.1602-1607). He served under Sultan Kalu Mohamed (#58 in our list = P51, third accession 1513-1529) or more likely Muhammad Imaduddine I (P68, reign 1632-1648) -- dates need reconciliation. Actually, Pyrard was in the Maldives 1602-1607, which falls during the Utheemu dynasty consolidation period.

Key offices documented:

| Office | Pyrard's Description | Modern Equivalent | Notes |
|--------|---------------------|-------------------|-------|
| **Bodubandeyri** | Chief Treasurer; administered criminal justice and gathered taxes alongside atoluverin and kateebs | Chief Minister/Treasurer | One of the highest court offices |
| **Fandiyaaru** | Royal Judge; topmost judicial official | Chief Justice | Implemented laws and religious hukm through subordinates |
| **Naibu (Naib)** | Every island had a naib as the chief justice's representative; in charge of criminal, civil, and religious justice | Island-level judge/magistrate | Now known as Qadi or Gazi |
| **Katib (Kateeb)** | Principal temporal and spiritual authority on each populated island; sultan's representative | Island chief/administrator | Combined administrative and religious role |
| **Atoluverin** | Atoll-level administrative chiefs | Atoll chief | Worked alongside bodubandeyri |
| **Mudim (Imam)** | Managed the mosques | Mosque imam | Religious role |

Pyrard noted the sultan held the title "Sultan Ibrahim dolos assa ral tera atholon" ("Ibrahim Sultan Roy of thirteen provinces and of twelve mil isles"), referenced also by Bell.

### 3.4 Court Structure and Hierarchy

Pyrard's account establishes a clear hierarchical governance model:
1. **Sultan** -- supreme authority, residing in Male
2. **Bodubandeyri** -- chief treasurer/minister, highest administrative officer
3. **Fandiyaaru** -- chief justice, highest judicial officer
4. **Atoluverin** -- atoll-level chiefs (13 atolls as administrative provinces)
5. **Naibu** -- island-level judicial representatives
6. **Katib** -- island-level administrative/spiritual representatives
7. **Mudim** -- mosque-level religious officials

The system integrated administrative, judicial, and religious authority. Pyrard's testimony on the 13-atoll administrative structure matches Bell's 1883 listing of the same 13 atolls.

### 3.5 Succession Practices Observed

Pyrard's account describes the role of the Bodubandeyri and other court officials in succession. He documents that the Utheemu brothers who drove away the Portuguese consisted of an elder named "Mahomet" (called "Bode ta Courou" meaning "great lord" = Bodu Thakurufaanu = Muhammad Thakurufaanu) and the younger called "Assan Quilague" (= Hassan Kilegefaanu). The elder brother's son later reigned as Ibrahim III Kalaafaan.

### 3.6 Named Individuals

| Person | Pyrard's Name | Identification | Notes |
|--------|---------------|----------------|-------|
| Bodu Thakurufaanu | "Mahomet" / "Bode ta Courou" | Muhammad Thakurufaanu al-Auzam (P104 in project) | National hero; elder of the Utheemu brothers |

### 3.7 Legal/Judicial System

Pyrard documented a structured legal system where criminal justice was administered by the Bodubandeyri, Atoluverin, and Kateebs who also gathered the **vaaru** (poll tax) and **varuvaa** (land tax). The Fandiyaaru presided over religious law (hukm), with Naibs and Kateebs implementing judgments island by island.

## 4. Dutch VOC Findings

### 4.1 GLOBALISE Database Results

The GLOBALISE database (globalise.huygens.knaw.nl) for digitized VOC records was not directly accessible for detailed extraction during this session. The Dutch-Maldives connection was primarily commercial rather than colonial.

### 4.2 Trade Records

The Dutch East India Company (VOC, formed 1602) engaged with the Maldives primarily through the cowrie shell and coir rope trade:
- **1602**: Dutch sent investigation to Maldives from Ceylon regarding trade potential
- **c.1640**: A vessel was sent from Ceylon to Maldives with rice to explore cowrie trading possibilities; trading began soon after
- **By 1670s**: Merchants in the Balasore area had 6-7 vessels regularly employed bringing cowries and coir from the Maldives
- The Dutch held a monopoly on cowrie trade between Maldives and Ceylon by late 17th century, lasting much of the 18th century
- Cowrie shells were a primary currency medium across Indian Ocean trade networks

### 4.3 Diplomatic Correspondence

No detailed diplomatic correspondence between Dutch officials and specific Maldivian sultans was found in this search. The Dutch relationship with the Maldives was mediated primarily through their Ceylon administration.

### 4.4 Named Maldivian Rulers Referenced

No specific named Maldivian rulers were identified in Dutch VOC records during this search.

## 5. British Colonial Findings

### 5.1 British Protectorate Period

- **1887**: Maldives became a British protectorate under an agreement with Sultan
- **1965 (July 26)**: Maldives achieved independence. Agreement signed by Ibrahim Nasir Rannabandeyri Kilegefan (Prime Minister) representing the King of the Maldives, and Sir Michael Walker (British Ambassador-designate) representing Queen Elizabeth II
- **1968**: Republic inaugurated and sultanate abolished

### 5.2 Late Sultanate Records

Key figures in the late British protectorate period:
- Sultan Muhammad Shamsuddeen III (P91, reigned 1903-1933) -- corresponds to British protectorate period
- Sultan Hassan Nooredine II (P94, reigned 1933-1943)
- Sultan Muhammad Farid I (P95, reigned 1954-1968) -- last sultan; declared himself King upon independence
- Mohamed Amin Didi -- President of the First Republic (1953-1954)
- Ibrahim Nasir -- Prime Minister who signed independence agreement

### 5.3 Archive References

- **FCO 141 series**: UK National Archives, colonial administration records (migrated archives). Maldives references exist but specific files not itemized in this search.
- **CO 167 series**: Ceylon administration files, which would cover Maldives as a dependency. Specific Maldives files not itemized.
- **Bell Family Papers**: Cambridge CSAS, 2 boxes of documents relating to archaeological work.

### 5.4 Governance Structure

The British protectorate arrangement left internal governance to the Maldivian sultan. The British role was primarily external affairs, defense, and trade. The 13-atoll administrative structure documented by Pyrard and Bell persisted through the protectorate period.

### 6.2 Genealogical Claims from Academic Sources

**Romero-Frias on the Koimala Legend**:
- King Koimala, a nobleman of the Lion Race from Sri Lanka, sailed to Rasgetheemu island (North Maalhosmadulu Atoll), then to Male, establishing Dheeva Mari kingdom
- A kingly dynasty from northern regions of the Subcontinent established power approximately two millennia ago, likely bringing Buddhist Dharma
- In 1153, Abu al-Barkat converted the last Buddhist monarch King Dhovemi to Islam

**Peacock on the Tarikh**:
- The Arabic chronicle (Tarikh) was composed by qadi Hasan Taj al-Din (d. 1139/1727), continued by nephew Muhammad Muhibb al-Din (1118/1706-1199/1785), and grandson Ibrahim Siraj al-Din (d. after 1243/1827)
- Chronicles reigns of sovereigns from 1153 to 1821
- Contains factional biases and religious convictions that veil certain political realities
- The Tarikh is the primary indigenous Arabic-language historical chronicle

### 6.3 The Malabar Interregnum and Huraagey Founding (1752-1759)

**Key sequence** (from multiple European-language sources):
1. 1752: Sultan Muhammad Imaduddin III (P79) seized by Ali Raja of Cannanore (Arakkal Kingdom, Kerala)
2. P79 transported to Kavaratti island in the Laccadives; died in captivity 1757
3. Male occupied by Malabar forces
4. The sultan's niece Amina I and daughter Amina II served as titular rulers during interregnum
5. De facto regent: **Muleegey Dom Hassan Maniku** (= P80, Hassan Izzuddine / Donbandaarain)
6. Described as "a direct descendant of the penultimate Christian King Joao" (= P66)
7. Dom Hassan Maniku expelled Ali Raja's forces
8. Installed as king Friday December 7, 1759 as "King Siri Kularanmeeba Audha Keerithi Katthiri Bavana (Hassan Izzuddine)" = **P80**
9. Died 1766, aged 35 (birth c.1731; project has yb:1728)
10. Founded the **Huraagey Dynasty** which ruled until 1968

## 10. Summary Assessment

### What We Now Know

1. **Bell's corpus** is larger and more important than the project currently reflects. The 1940 monograph is THE missing cornerstone source. The Excerpta Maldiviana (1922-1926) contains translated historical documents that could resolve many current uncertainties. Both should be registered as P0 priority sources.

2. **The Portuguese period** is far more genealogically complex than the project represents. The RoyalArk genealogy reveals at least 8-10 additional named persons in the Dom Manoel through Dom Luis lineage who should be modeled as nodes, including Dom Francisco, Dom Pedro, Dona Leonor, Dona Catarina, Dona Ines, Dona Francisca de Vasconcelos, Captain Sebastiao Tavares de Souza, and Dom Luis de Souza.

3. **The Hilaaly-to-Huraagey bridge** is better documented than project edges suggest. The 5-generation chain (P66 → Dona Ines → Dom Luis → Dom Maraduru → Hussain Daharada → Mohamed Faamuladeyri → P80) has intermediate persons that could be added as nodes.

4. **Pyrard's testimony** on court offices and governance structure is Grade A evidence that feeds the title/office concept register and provides the earliest detailed European description of the Fandiyaaru, Bodubandeyri, Naib, and Katib offices.

5. **The St. Francis Xavier baptism claim** for P61 is not directly corroborated by European sources found in this search and should be flagged for further investigation.

6. **Chandra de Silva's "Portuguese Encounters"** (2009) is a crucial unregistered source containing translated primary Portuguese texts specifically covering the Maldives in Chapter 7.

7. **The Maldives Heritage Survey** represents the most comprehensive modern archaeological work and could provide inscriptional corroboration for early dynastic claims.
