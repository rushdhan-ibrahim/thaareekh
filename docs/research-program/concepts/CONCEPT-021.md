# Concept Entry

Concept ID: `CONCEPT-021`
Last updated: `2026-02-09`
Category: `political concept`

## 1) Canonical label
- Primary label: Exile and provincial power
- Alternate labels/spellings: Political exile to outer atolls; banishment; provincial aristocracy; southern elite formation.
- Language/script forms: Dhivehi terminology for exile/banishment; English "exile", "banishment", "provincial power"; the concept is described in chronicle and secondary sources but may lack a single Dhivehi technical term.

## 2) Definition
- Short definition: The use of exile to outer atolls as a political instrument in the Maldivian sultanate, whereby deposed rulers and their families were banished to distant atolls (especially Fuvahmulah, Addu, and Huvadhu), where they and their descendants formed a provincial aristocracy that maintained power networks and genealogical claims across generations.
- Historical scope and periodization:
  - **Sultanate-era practice**: Exile to outer atolls was a standard political tool used by victorious factions to neutralize rivals without execution. The remoteness of the southern atolls (Fuvahmulah, Addu) made them effective exile destinations.
  - **Isdu dynasty exile (1704)**: Sultan Ibrahim Mudzhiruddine (`P76`) of the Isdu dynasty was deposed and exiled to Fuvahmulah by his cousin Sultan Muhammad Imaduddin II (`P77`) of the Dhiyamigili dynasty. His descendants still live in Fuvahmulah and neighboring atolls.
  - **Dhiyamigili exile**: Mohamed Ghiyathuddine (`P81`) was deposed while on Hajj (c. 1774), and Dhiyamigili-connected families were subsequently dispersed to outer atolls.
  - **Southern aristocracy formation**: Over centuries, the accumulation of exiled royal families in the southern atolls created a distinct southern aristocracy -- the "intelligentsia and descendants of exiled kings" who maintained genealogical claims to royal status.
  - **United Suvadive Republic (1959-1963)**: The southern atolls' independent political identity, shaped partly by the presence of exiled royal descendants, culminated in a secession attempt. Addu, Huvadhu, and Fuvahmulah declared independence, partly motivated by resentment of Male centralization.
- Why it matters in this genealogy graph: Exile explains the geographic distribution of royal lineage claims, the formation of the southern branch narratives in our data, and the existence of genealogically significant persons in atolls far from Male.

## 3) Semantic and historical notes
- Exile was not a death sentence but a political neutralization. Exiled royals often maintained status, married locally, and produced descendants who claimed royal heritage.
- The southern aristocracy is documented in the Maldives Royal Family records (Addu/Meedhoo branch), providing genealogical links between exiled rulers and modern descendants.
- Exile destinations were chosen for remoteness: Fuvahmulah and Addu are the southernmost inhabited islands, roughly 500+ km from Male.
- The resentment generated by exile and Male centralization contributed to periodic southern resistance, culminating in the Suvadive secession.
- Cross-reference with CONCEPT-018 (Atoll governance) for how atoll chiefs administered exile destinations, and CONCEPT-001 (Dynastic succession logic) for how exile affected succession claims.
- The graph data includes southern branch persons (P130, P132, P182, P191) whose presence in Addu/Meedhoo is explained by exile history.
