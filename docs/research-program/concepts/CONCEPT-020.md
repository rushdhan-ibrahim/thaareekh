# Concept Entry

Concept ID: `CONCEPT-020`
Last updated: `2026-02-09`
Category: `institution`

## 1) Canonical label
- Primary label: Military and naval institutions
- Alternate labels/spellings: Dhivehi navy; liberation wars; Dhoshimeynaa (military portfolio); maritime defense; Shahbandar (navy/admiral portfolio).
- Language/script forms: Dhivehi *Dhoshimeynaa* (ދޮށިމެދު, military/army-general office); *Shahbandar* (ޝަހެބަންދަރު, navy/admiral portfolio); English "military", "navy", "garrison".

## 2) Definition
- Short definition: The military and naval institutions of the Maldivian sultanate, encompassing the Dhoshimeynaa office (army/defense portfolio), maritime defense against foreign invasions, the liberation campaigns against Portuguese rule, and the broader naval/garrison system protecting an archipelagic state.
- Historical scope and periodization:
  - **Pre-Portuguese**: The Dhoshimeynaa (OFF-DHOSHIMEYNAA) is attested as a military-linked office in the Furadaana from at least the Pyrard account (c. 1602-1607), described as army-general/chief defense portfolio. Maritime defense was essential for an archipelagic state dependent on sea routes.
  - **Portuguese invasion and occupation (1558-1573)**: Portuguese forces invaded under Captain Andiri Andirin, occupying the Maldives for 15 years, imposing Christianity, and establishing a garrison. This is the central military crisis of Maldivian history.
  - **Liberation campaign (1565-1573)**: Muhammad Thakurufaanu (`P104`) and his brothers Ali (`P102`) and Hassan (`P103`) waged an eight-year naval guerrilla campaign from their vessel, the *Kalhuohfummi*. They operated from Minicoy island and conducted nighttime raids across the atolls. The final assault on Male on July 2, 1573 killed the Portuguese Captain-General and liberated the country.
  - **Post-liberation military**: The Utheemu and later dynasties maintained naval capabilities and the Dhoshimeynaa office. The Shahbandar (port/navy portfolio) also had military dimensions.
  - **Late monarchy/modern**: Military institutions evolved into the modern Maldives National Defence Force. The liberation of 1573 remains the foundational military narrative.
- Why it matters in this genealogy graph: Military events (invasions, liberations, coups) are the primary triggers for dynastic transitions. Understanding military institutions helps explain how non-royal military leaders could seize power and found new dynasties.

## 3) Semantic and historical notes
- The Dhoshimeynaa office was part of the Furadaana council (CONCEPT-002) and represented the defense/military portfolio. Its holder was described as army-general in various periods.
- The Shahbandar combined port master and naval functions. In the Maldivian context, the Shahbandar was described as holding the navy portfolio and serving as admiral-in-chief. Cross-reference with CONCEPT-027 for the Shahbandar's trade/port functions.
- The *Kalhuohfummi* (Muhammad Thakurufaanu's vessel) represents the guerrilla naval warfare approach suited to an archipelagic state: fast, light vessels that could operate at night and evade larger Portuguese ships.
- Ali Thakurufaanu (`P102`) was killed during the liberation campaign, making him a martyr figure alongside his brother Muhammad's hero status.
- Military events are closely tied to succession: the Portuguese invasion deposed the existing Hilaaly line; the liberation installed the Utheemu dynasty; later coups and depositions often involved military force.
- The Dhoshimeynaa office was distinct from the sovereign's personal military command; the relationship between the two requires further research.
