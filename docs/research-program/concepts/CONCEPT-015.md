# Concept Entry

Concept ID: `CONCEPT-015`
Last updated: `2026-02-09`
Category: `political concept`

## 1) Canonical label
- Primary label: Regency and guardianship
- Alternate labels/spellings: Regent rule; child-sultan governance; rule in absentia; *Shanuraza* (regent/commander-in-chief, attested title).
- Language/script forms: Dhivehi *henevirasun* (later transcription of *Shanuraza*); English "regent"; Arabic *naib* (deputy) sometimes overlaps with regent function.

## 2) Definition
- Short definition: The institutional mechanism by which an adult authority exercises sovereign power on behalf of a minor, absent, or incapacitated ruler, a recurring pattern in Maldivian history where several sultans acceded as children or governed from abroad.
- Historical scope and periodization:
  - **Lunar dynasty**: No clear regency cases attested, though the rapid succession of short-reigning sultans in the 13th century may imply informal guardianship arrangements.
  - **Hilaaly dynasty**: Multiple depositions and restorations (e.g., `P31` Ibrahim deposed by uncle, later returned) suggest power-sharing arrangements that functioned as de facto regencies.
  - **Portuguese period (1558-1573)**: The regent Andiri Andirin governed the Maldives on behalf of Portuguese interests while Hassan IX (`P61`) was in Goa. This is the most explicitly attested regency in the historical record.
  - **Goa-based rule (1583-1632)**: Kings Joao (`P66`) and Philippe (`P67`) ruled from Goa through local regents. Ibrahim Kalaafaan served as de facto ruler during this period.
  - **Utheemu period**: Sultan Kuda Mohamed (`P70`, r. 1687-1691) was a child ruler, implying regency or guardianship by court officials.
  - **Late monarchy**: Sultan Mohamed Imaduddine V (`P90`, r. 1892) was an infant whose brief reign required regent authority.
- Why it matters in this genealogy graph: Regent relationships create edges that look like kinship but may reflect political appointment rather than blood ties. The graph must distinguish guardian/ward relationships from parent/child.

## 3) Semantic and historical notes
- The *Shanuraza* title (later transcribed as *henevirasun*) was described as the most senior official after the king, meaning regent or commander-in-chief. This office may have formalized regency functions.
- Do not automatically infer parentage from regent-ward proximity. A regent may be an uncle, cousin, court official, or foreign appointee.
- The Goa-based regency (1583-1632) is unique in Maldivian history as it represents rule in absentia over decades, with the sovereign residing in a foreign territory.
- Regency episodes are often associated with contested succession (see CONCEPT-001), as rival factions may install a minor as figurehead while exercising real power.
- Cross-reference with CONCEPT-025 (Bodu Vizier) for cases where the chief minister effectively served as regent during weak sultanates.

- Travel narratives (Pyrard) describe the Goa-based absentee rule and local regent arrangements.
- Chronicle sources (Tarikh) record succession events involving minors and depositions that imply regency.
