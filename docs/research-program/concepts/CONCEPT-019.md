# Concept Entry

Concept ID: `CONCEPT-019`
Last updated: `2026-02-09`
Category: `institution`

## 1) Canonical label
- Primary label: Friday Mosque / Hukuru Miskiiy
- Alternate labels/spellings: Male Friday Mosque; Old Friday Mosque; *Hukuru Miskiy* (ހުކުރު މިސްކިތް); Male Hukuru Miskiy; Grand Friday Mosque.
- Language/script forms: Dhivehi *Hukuru Miskiy* (Friday Mosque); Arabic *masjid al-jum'a* (Friday mosque); English "Friday Mosque" or "Old Friday Mosque".

## 2) Definition
- Short definition: The central religious-ceremonial institution of the Maldivian sultanate, tied to sovereign legitimacy, investiture ceremonies, and the physical expression of Islamic governance. The current coral stone building (completed 1658) replaced an earlier mosque from 1153.
- Historical scope and periodization:
  - **1153 CE**: First mosque built by the first Muslim sultan, Mohamed bin Abdullah (Sultan Dhovemi `P2` after conversion), marking the Islamization of the Maldives and establishing the Friday Mosque as the institutional center of Islamic governance.
  - **1656-1658**: The current building constructed under Sultan Ibrahim Iskandhar I (`P69`, r. 1648-1687), replacing the original 12th-century structure. Built from interlocking coral stone blocks with lacquer-work interior.
  - **Sultanate period**: The mosque served as the primary venue for Friday prayers led by or in the name of the sultan, reinforcing the sovereign's religious authority. The *khutba* (Friday sermon) traditionally included the ruler's name, symbolizing legitimacy.
  - **UNESCO tentative listing (2008)**: The coral stone mosques of the Maldives, including Hukuru Miskiiy, were added to the UNESCO World Heritage tentative list as unique examples of sea-culture architecture.
- Why it matters in this genealogy graph: The Friday Mosque is the institutional space where sovereign legitimacy was publicly performed. Construction patronage, khutba naming, and burial in the mosque precinct are all markers of sovereign status that contextualise person nodes.

## 3) Semantic and historical notes
- The mosque's construction from Porites coral stone represents a uniquely Maldivian architectural tradition. The coral is soft and easily carved when wet, but hardens when dry, enabling the intricate lacquer-work and calligraphic panels that distinguish Maldivian mosque architecture.
- The mosque precinct contains the tombs of several sultans, making it both a religious and memorial site. Burial location in or near the Hukuru Miskiiy was a marker of sovereign status.
- The *Munnaaru* (minaret) adjacent to the mosque is a separate coral stone structure, also dating from the sultanate period.
- The Fandiyaaru (CONCEPT-010) had oversight of mosque maintenance and religious rituals, connecting judicial and religious authority.
- Ibrahim Iskandhar I (`P69`) built the current mosque, making his patronage a key marker of Utheemu-dynasty legitimacy. The mosque thus embodies the shift from pre-Islamic Buddhist-era religious architecture to Islamic institutional space.
- Cross-reference with CONCEPT-029 (Conversion and religious legitimacy) for the mosque's role in the broader narrative of Islamic legitimation.

## 5) Evidence
- The physical building survives and is accessible for architectural and epigraphic analysis.
- Inscriptions within the mosque (coral stone panels, lacquer-work calligraphy) provide primary evidence of patronage and dating.
- The UNESCO tentative listing documentation provides architectural and historical assessment.
- Travel narratives (Pyrard, Bell) describe the mosque and its functions during their visits.
