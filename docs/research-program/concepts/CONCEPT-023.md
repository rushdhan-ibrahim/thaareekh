# Concept Entry

Concept ID: `CONCEPT-023`
Last updated: `2026-02-09`
Category: `system`

## 1) Canonical label
- Primary label: Pre-Islamic / Buddhist-era continuities
- Alternate labels/spellings: Buddhist Maldives; Koimala legend; Solar and Lunar dynasties; Sinhala/South Indian connections; Buddhist-to-Islamic transition.
- Language/script forms: Dhivehi *Dharumavantha* (the Buddhist-era royal style); Sanskrit/Pali-derived terminology in regnal names; English "pre-Islamic", "Buddhist era".

## 2) Definition
- Short definition: The pre-Islamic cultural, institutional, and genealogical substrate of the Maldives, including the Buddhist-era ruling traditions, the Koimala founding legend, the Solar and Lunar dynasty framework, and the archaeological/linguistic evidence for continuity between Buddhist and Islamic-era governance.
- Historical scope and periodization:
  - **Buddhist period (c. 3rd century BCE - 1153 CE)**: Buddhism spread to the Maldives in the 3rd century BCE during the time of Ashoka. Buddhist monasteries, stupas, and inscriptions have been found across the archipelago (notably by Thor Heyerdahl and other archaeologists). The Buddhist period lasted approximately 1,400 years.
  - **Solar and Lunar dynasties**: Traditional Maldivian historiography divides pre-Islamic rulers into Solar (*Aadheeththa*) and Lunar (*Soma*) dynasty lines. Queen Srimati Damahara, the last Solar dynasty ruler, married Prince Sri Baladitya, who founded the Lunar dynasty. This dual-dynasty tradition may reflect broader Indian Ocean royal genealogical conventions.
  - **Koimala legend**: Koimala (`P1`, Maha Parama/Maanaabarana, r. 1117-1141) is the legendary founder-figure who arrived from Ceylon and established rule in Male. The name "Koimala" (or "Kaimal") may be a title given to Keralese noblemen, suggesting a Malabar Coast connection. He unified the archipelago's atolls under a single kingdom.
  - **Conversion (1153 CE)**: King Dhovemi (`P2`), nephew of Koimala, converted to Islam and took the title Sultan Muhammad al-Adil and the name Dharumavantha Rasgefaanu. The conversion is traditionally attributed to Abu al-Barakat Yusuf al-Barbari. See CONCEPT-029 for detailed conversion analysis.
  - **Post-conversion continuities**: Many pre-Islamic elements persisted after 1153: Indic-origin regnal name tokens (CONCEPT-014), matrilineal succession patterns, coral stone architectural traditions, and the Solar/Lunar dynasty framework itself.
- Why it matters in this genealogy graph: The pre-Islamic substrate explains the non-Islamic elements in the graph data (Indic regnal names, matrilineal claims, the Solar/Lunar dynasty labels) and provides context for the earliest nodes.

## 3) Semantic and historical notes
- The Koimala legend is foundational but historically uncertain. "Koimala" may be a title (Keralese *Kaimal*), not a personal name. The legend's claim of Ceylonese origin may reflect political legitimation rather than historical migration.
- Archaeological evidence of pre-Islamic Buddhism in the Maldives includes: coral stone Buddhist temples (converted to mosques), Buddhist sculptures and reliefs (many destroyed during Islamization), Dhivehi Akuru script inscriptions, and coral stone monasteries on various islands.
- The mandatory conversion of all subjects to Islam, decreed after 1153, eliminated religious pluralism and destroyed most Buddhist-era material culture. This makes archaeological evidence particularly valuable.
- Pre-Islamic matrilineal patterns may explain the later female sultanates (CONCEPT-022) and the persistence of maternal-line succession claims.
- The Indic-origin regnal name tokens (CONCEPT-014) provide linguistic evidence for Buddhist-era cultural continuity: terms like *Bavana*, *Loka*, *Abarana*, *Maha Radun* derive from Sanskrit/Pali and were retained in Islamic-era royal naming.
- The graph's earliest nodes (`P1` Koimala, `P2` Dhovemi) straddle the Buddhist-Islamic transition and require careful handling of source traditions.

## 5) Evidence
- Archaeological surveys (Heyerdahl, Bell) provide material evidence for pre-Islamic Buddhism.
- The Dhivehi Akuru script inscriptions are primary evidence for pre-Islamic literary culture.
- The Tarikh and Raadhavalhi preserve the traditional Solar/Lunar dynasty narrative.
- Linguistic analysis of regnal name tokens provides evidence for Indic-layer cultural continuity.
- Loamaafaanu copper-plate grants provide early inscriptional evidence.
