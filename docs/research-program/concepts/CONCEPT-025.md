# Concept Entry

Concept ID: `CONCEPT-025`
Last updated: `2026-02-09`
Category: `office/institution`

## 1) Canonical label
- Primary label: Bodu Vizier / Grand Vizier institution
- Alternate labels/spellings: *Bodu Vazirin*; *Boduvazirin* (ބޮޑުވަޒީރު); Prime Minister of the Sultanate; Chief Minister; *Wazir* (Arabic).
- Language/script forms: Dhivehi *Bodu Vazirin* (Grand Vizier / Prime Minister); Arabic *wazir* (وزير, minister) with Dhivehi *bodu* (big/great); English "Grand Vizier", "Prime Minister".

## 2) Definition
- Short definition: The chief minister role in the Maldivian sultanate, where the Bodu Vizier held effective executive power, particularly during periods of weak or absent sultans, evolving from the Islamic *wazir* concept into a distinctly Maldivian institutional office.
- Historical scope and periodization:
  - **Early attestation**: The vizier concept entered Maldivian governance with Islamization (post-1153), drawing on the broader Islamic tradition of the *wazir* as chief adviser to the ruler.
  - **Ibn Battuta era (c. 1340s)**: The vizier is attested as a powerful figure in Khadijah's court. Ibn Battuta describes the political dynamics between the sultana and her viziers/ministers.
  - **Pyrard account (c. 1602-1607)**: The Bodu Vizier (or equivalent chief minister) is described as head of the cabinet ministers, with various *vazierin* (ministers) holding specific portfolios including Hakuraa (Public Works), Shahbandar (Navy), and Velaanaa (Foreign Affairs).
  - **Late monarchy**: The Bodu Vizier became effectively a Prime Minister, and during weak sultanates the chief minister could be the de facto ruler. The term *henevirasun* (regent/commander-in-chief) sometimes overlapped with vizier function (see CONCEPT-015).
  - **Constitutional era (1932-1968)**: The office became more formalized as the constitutional framework developed. Several Bodu Viziers were powerful political figures who influenced succession.
  - **Republican transformation**: The vizier role was replaced by modern prime ministerial/presidential executive functions.
- Why it matters in this genealogy graph: The Bodu Vizier was often a close relative of the sultan (son, brother, nephew, cousin) or married into the royal family. Vizier-to-sultan transitions are among the most important succession events in the graph.

## 3) Semantic and historical notes
- The Bodu Vizier was not merely an adviser but often held effective executive power, especially when the sultan was weak, absent (see CONCEPT-015 on regency), or a child.
- Some key persons in the graph held vizier-like positions before ascending to the throne: Osman of Fehendu (`P29`) served as PM to `P25` and `P27` before possibly claiming influence. Several Hilaaly-era figures served as ministers before seizing the throne.
- The ministerial cabinet under the Bodu Vizier included specialized portfolios: Hakuraa (Public Works), Shahbandar (Navy/admiral), Velaanaa (Foreign Affairs), and others documented in the Furadaana structure (CONCEPT-002).
- The Chief Justice (Fandiyaaru, CONCEPT-010) ranked before the great offices of state in some accounts, creating a potential tension between judicial and executive authority.
- Cross-reference with CONCEPT-015 (Regency) for cases where the vizier functioned as regent.

## 5) Evidence
- The Pyrard account provides detailed description of the vizier and ministerial cabinet structure.
- Suood's legal-historical analysis documents the evolution of the executive office.
- Chronicle sources record vizier names and their relationships to sultans.
- Modern political histories of the Maldives document the late-monarchy prime ministerial dynamics.
