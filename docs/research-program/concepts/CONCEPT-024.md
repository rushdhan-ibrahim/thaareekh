# Concept Entry

Concept ID: `CONCEPT-024`
Last updated: `2026-02-09`
Category: `evidence`

## 1) Canonical label
- Primary label: Maldivian numismatic evidence
- Alternate labels/spellings: Larin coinage; cowrie currency; *laari* (Dhivehi monetary unit); *rufiyaa*; Maldivian coins; sultan-name coins.
- Language/script forms: Dhivehi *laari* (ލާރި, monetary unit); *rufiyaa* (ރުފިޔާ, currency); Persian/Arabic *larin* (لارین, silver wire currency); English "cowrie", "larin", "coin".

## 2) Definition
- Short definition: The numismatic evidence available for the Maldivian sultanate, including cowrie shells (the earliest currency), silver larin coins bearing sultan names and Islamic dates, gold coinage, and later machine-struck coins -- all of which provide independent dating and attestation evidence for rulers in the genealogy.
- Historical scope and periodization:
  - **Pre-monetary and cowrie era (pre-13th century through 17th century)**: Cowrie shells (*Cypraea moneta*) were the primary currency of the Maldives and a major export commodity. Ibn Battuta (c. 1344) recorded that over 40 ships per year were loaded with cowrie shells for export. A single gold dinar was worth 400,000 shells.
  - **Larin importation (17th-18th century)**: Silver wire larin (parallel straps of silver wire folded in half, bearing Persian and Arabic inscriptions) were imported from the Persian Gulf and used as currency in the Indian Ocean trade network.
  - **Maldivian-minted larin (c. 1585-1685)**: The first domestically struck *dhigu laari* appeared during the reign of Sultan Ibrahim III ibn Ghazi Muhammad (`P67`, r. 1585-1607 approximately). Under Sultan Ibrahim Iskandhar I (`P69`, r. 1648-1687), silver larin coins bearing the sultan's name and Islamic dates were minted between 1660 and 1685, directly attesting to that ruler's sovereignty.
  - **Gold coinage (1787)**: Sultan Hassan Nooruddin I (`P84`, r. 1779-1799) introduced gold mohur and half-mohur coins in 1787, using high-purity gold.
  - **Machine-struck coins (1900s)**: Sultan Mohamed Imaduddine IV (`P92`) introduced the first machine-struck coins. Sultan Mohamed Shamsuddine III (`P91`) had 1 and 4 laari coins struck at Heaton's Mint, Birmingham, England, in 1913.
  - **Modern (1947+)**: Rufiyaa banknotes introduced in 1947; laari coins (1/100th of rufiyaa) introduced in 1960.
- Why it matters in this genealogy graph: Coins bearing sultan names and dates provide independent, material evidence for ruler identity and chronology. Numismatic attestation can corroborate or challenge chronological claims in the chronicle sources.

## 3) Semantic and historical notes
- Coins bearing a sultan's name and date are among the strongest independent evidence for a ruler's historicity. They should be treated as high-confidence attestation sources.
- The transition from cowrie shells to minted coins reflects the Maldives' integration into broader Indian Ocean trade networks. Cowrie exports connected the Maldives to Bengal, East Africa, and Southeast Asia.
- The larin-to-rufiyaa transition parallels the sultanate-to-republic political transition; monetary sovereignty is a marker of political sovereignty.
- Numismatic evidence may reveal rulers not well-attested in chronicle sources, or confirm the chronology of rulers whose reign dates are disputed.
- The gold mohur of Hassan Nooruddin I (`P84`) is particularly notable as it represents a claim to substantial sovereignty and wealth.
- Cross-reference with CONCEPT-027 (Shahbandar) for the trade context in which Maldivian coinage circulated.

## 5) Evidence
- Physical coin specimens in museum and private collections provide material evidence.
- Numismatic catalogs (e.g., Numista, Mintage World) document known coin types, dates, and ruler attributions.
- The coin inscriptions (sultan names, Islamic dates, Shahada) are primary text sources independent of chronicle tradition.
- Cowrie trade is documented in travel narratives (Ibn Battuta, Pyrard) and trade records.
