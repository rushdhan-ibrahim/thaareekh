# Concept Entry

Concept ID: `CONCEPT-010`
Last updated: `2026-02-09`
Category: `office/institution`

## 1) Canonical label
- Primary label: Fandiyaaru (Chief Judge)
- Alternate labels/spellings: Fandiyaar; Fandiyard; Qadi (Arabic equivalent); Chief Justice of the Sultanate.
- Language/script forms: Dhivehi *fandiyaaru* (ފަނޑިޔާރު); Arabic *qadi* (قاضي); English "Chief Judge" or "Chief Justice" in colonial-era translations.

## 2) Definition
- Short definition: The chief judicial office in the Maldivian sultanate, combining Islamic qadi functions (Sharia adjudication) with state-chronicle record-keeping and oversight of religious institutions.
- Historical scope and periodization:
  - **Ibn Battuta era (c. 1340s)**: Attested as a structured judicial office within the court cabinet, distinct from the ministerial Furadaana. Ibn Battuta himself served as qadi of the Maldives during his 1343-1345 sojourn.
  - **Pyrard account (c. 1602-1607)**: Listed among the principal offices of the Furadaana, with the Fandiyaaru described as holding authority over civil and ecclesiastical justice, mosque maintenance, charitable trusts, religious rituals, and the recording of the *tarikh* (state chronicle).
  - **Christopher account (1834)**: Fandiyaaru retained as distinct judicial office alongside expanded ministerial cabinet.
  - **Bell account (1879)**: Compressed government model still includes Chief Judge as one of few key offices.
  - **Constitutional era (1932-1968)**: Legal centralization reduced older judicial autonomy; Fandiyaaru role evolved toward a modern chief justice function.
  - **Republican era (1968-present)**: Traditional office title persists in social memory while formal judicial authority is under modern court structures.
- Why it matters in this genealogy graph: Fandiyaaru-linked language can inflate perceived lineage authority if not separated from kinship proof. Persons described as Fandiyaaru holders gain political weight but this does not confirm family ties.

## 3) Semantic and historical notes
- Model Fandiyaaru references as office semantics first, and only then map relation implications when direct wording exists.
- Where sources disagree on exact hierarchy (chief judge vs broad legal-religious office), retain ambiguity in notes rather than force a single definition.
- The Fandiyaaru ranked before the great offices of state in some accounts and held charge over the recording of state chronicles, making this office critical for source provenance.
- The office had authority over marriage, inheritance, and property disputes under Sharia, connecting it directly to CONCEPT-016 (Islamic legal succession framework).
- Judicial-office proximity should not be used as a shortcut for promoting inferred family edges.
- Ibn Battuta's personal tenure as qadi provides the earliest detailed account of judicial functions and their intersection with political authority.
