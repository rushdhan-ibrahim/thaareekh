# Concept Entry

Concept ID: `CONCEPT-016`
Last updated: `2026-02-09`
Category: `system`

## 1) Canonical label
- Primary label: Islamic legal succession framework
- Alternate labels/spellings: Sharia inheritance vs political succession; Islamic succession law in the Maldives; Fara'id (Islamic inheritance portions).
- Language/script forms: Arabic *fara'id* (فرائض, obligatory inheritance shares); Dhivehi legal-religious terminology for inheritance; English "Sharia succession".

## 2) Definition
- Short definition: The interaction between Islamic inheritance law (which prescribes fixed shares for heirs) and political succession customs (which follow power, appointment, and dynastic claim), creating a dual-track system where legal heirs and political successors may differ.
- Historical scope and periodization:
  - **Post-conversion (1153+)**: Adoption of Islamic law introduced Sharia inheritance principles, but political succession did not follow strict fara'id rules. The throne passed through various mechanisms: primogeniture, lateral succession to brothers, seizure, and even matrilineal claims.
  - **Ibn Battuta era (c. 1340s)**: The Fandiyaaru (qadi) administered Islamic law including inheritance cases, but political succession operated separately under customary rules.
  - **Female succession**: Sharia inheritance gives women half the male share, but the Maldives had female sovereigns (`P20`, `P25`, `P27`) -- a practice unusual in the Islamic world and not derived from Sharia.
  - **Late monarchy (1932 Constitution)**: Codified succession criteria that mixed Islamic requirements (Sunni Muslim) with dynastic criteria (descended from `P80`) and secular qualifications (literate, healthy, male aged 17+). This was a constitutional framework distinct from pure Sharia.
  - **Republican era (1968+)**: Sharia continues to govern personal-status law (marriage, inheritance, divorce) but political succession is through democratic election.
- Why it matters in this genealogy graph: Understanding the gap between Sharia inheritance law and actual succession helps explain why succession did not follow strict patrilineal primogeniture, and why women, lateral relatives, and even non-royal claimants could access the throne.

## 3) Semantic and historical notes
- Sharia inheritance law (fara'id) prescribes fixed shares: sons receive double the share of daughters; specific fractions go to parents, spouses, and other relatives. This applies to property, not automatically to the throne.
- Political succession in the Maldives was never purely Sharia-governed. It combined elements of: Islamic legitimacy (ruler must be Muslim), dynastic custom (preference for the ruling house), meritocratic selection (administrative talent, health), and political settlement (coup, deposition, negotiation).
- The frequent lateral successions (brother-to-brother, uncle-to-nephew) in the Lunar and Hilaaly dynasties suggest pre-Islamic matrilineal or cognatic succession patterns that persisted after Islamization.
- The Fandiyaaru (CONCEPT-010) had authority over inheritance disputes but not over political succession, which was settled by political and military means.
- Cross-reference with CONCEPT-001 (Dynastic succession logic) for how these principles play out in specific transitions.

## 5) Evidence
- Suood's legal-historical analysis provides the primary framework for understanding Islamic law in the Maldives.
- The Emory Islamic Family Law project documents Maldivian personal-status law and its Sharia basis.
- Constitutional texts (1932, 1968) codify the transition from customary-Islamic to constitutional succession.
- Chronicle sources record actual succession patterns that deviate from strict Sharia inheritance.
