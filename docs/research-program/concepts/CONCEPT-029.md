# Concept Entry

Concept ID: `CONCEPT-029`
Last updated: `2026-02-09`
Category: `system`

## 1) Canonical label
- Primary label: Conversion and religious legitimacy
- Alternate labels/spellings: Islamization; religious legitimation; hajj pilgrimage and prestige; Abu al-Barakat tradition; Sufi influence; Islamic conversion narrative.
- Language/script forms: Arabic *shahada* (شهادة, declaration of faith); Dhivehi *Dharumavantha* (the righteous one, title of first Muslim sultan); English "conversion", "Islamization", "religious legitimacy".

## 2) Definition
- Short definition: The conversion of the Maldives to Islam (traditionally dated 1153 CE) and the ongoing role of Islamic faith as a source of political legitimacy, encompassing the founding conversion narrative, the hajj pilgrimage as a prestige mechanism, Sufi influences, and the constitutional requirement of Islamic faith for sovereign authority.
- Historical scope and periodization:
  - **Pre-conversion**: Buddhism was the state religion for approximately 1,400 years (c. 3rd century BCE - 1153 CE). Arabic and Persian traders had been present in the Indian Ocean since at least the 9th century, introducing Islamic ideas through commerce.
  - **Conversion event (1153 CE)**: The last Buddhist king, Dhovemi (`P2`), converted to Islam and took the title Sultan Muhammad al-Adil and the name *Dharumavantha Rasgefaanu*. The conversion is attributed to Abu al-Barakat Yusuf al-Barbari (or al-Tamimi), described variously as a Moroccan/Berber missionary, a Somali scholar, or a Tabriz-origin traveler.
  - **Mandatory conversion**: Following the king's conversion, all subjects were required to convert to Islam, ending religious pluralism. Buddhist temples were converted to mosques; Buddhist sculptures and relics were destroyed.
  - **Islamic legitimacy framework**: From 1153 onward, Islamic faith became a prerequisite for political authority. The sultan's legitimacy was tied to his role as protector of Islam, expressed through the khutba (Friday sermon naming), mosque patronage, and enforcement of Sharia.
  - **Hajj pilgrimage**: Hajj to Mecca served as a prestige mechanism for sultans and elite figures. Several rulers traveled on Hajj, sometimes with political consequences (e.g., `P81` Mohamed Ghiyathuddine was deposed while on Hajj; `P76` Ibrahim Mudzhiruddine was deposed while on Hajj).
  - **Sufi influence**: Early Maldivian Islam incorporated Sufi practices and rituals alongside Buddhist-era *Eveyla* traditions and orthodox Sunni observance. The Abu al-Barakat tradition may itself reflect Sufi missionary activity. However, Islamic mysticism and Sufi ideas later became officially disapproved.
  - **Constitutional requirement**: The 1932 Constitution required the sovereign to be a Sunni Muslim, formalizing what had been customary since 1153.
- Why it matters in this genealogy graph: Religious legitimacy is a background condition for all sovereign nodes after 1153. The conversion narrative provides the foundational legitimacy claim; subsequent Islamic practice (hajj, mosque patronage, Sharia enforcement) sustained it. Nodes that lack Islamic credentials may have weaker legitimacy claims.

## 3) Semantic and historical notes
- The Abu al-Barakat conversion narrative exists in multiple versions:
  - **Moroccan/Berber**: Abu al-Barakat Yusuf al-Barbari, a Moroccan traveler, converted the king through teaching and persuasion.
  - **Somali**: Some traditions identify the missionary as Somali rather than Moroccan.
  - **Tabriz origin**: Other accounts give a Tabriz (Persian) origin.
  - The multiplicity of origin narratives suggests the conversion may have involved multiple Islamic influences rather than a single missionary event.
- The conversion was likely influenced by the growing dominance of Arab/Muslim traders in the Indian Ocean by the 12th century. Economic incentives (access to trade networks) may have complemented religious persuasion.
- Hajj pilgrimage was politically risky for sultans: absence from Male created power vacuums. At least two sultans (`P76` Ibrahim Mudzhiruddine, `P81` Mohamed Ghiyathuddine) were deposed while away on Hajj, suggesting that rivals exploited the absence.
- Sufi influence in Maldivian Islam is understudied. The early amalgamation of Islamic teachings, Buddhist Eveyla traditions, and Sufi practices created a syncretic religious culture that was later suppressed in favor of orthodox Sunni observance.
- The destruction of Buddhist-era material culture during Islamization eliminates evidence for the pre-Islamic period (see CONCEPT-023), making the conversion a critical source gap.
- Cross-reference with CONCEPT-023 (Pre-Islamic / Buddhist-era continuities) for what was lost during conversion, and CONCEPT-019 (Friday Mosque) for the institutional expression of Islamic legitimacy.

## 5) Evidence
- The Tarikh chronicles provide the primary narrative of the conversion event.
- Ibn Battuta's Rihla provides a contemporary account of Islamic practice in the Maldives (c. 1343-1345).
- The Abu al-Barakat tradition is preserved in multiple (sometimes conflicting) versions across sources.
- Archaeological evidence of destroyed Buddhist temples converted to mosques provides material evidence for the conversion process.
- The Friday Mosque (CONCEPT-019) is physical evidence of Islamic institutional establishment.

## 7) Open questions
- Can the Abu al-Barakat conversion narrative be historicized? Is there independent evidence for a specific missionary figure, or is the tradition legendary?
- What role did economic incentives (trade access) play in the conversion, compared to religious persuasion?
- How did Sufi practices interact with local Buddhist Eveyla traditions in the early Islamic period? Is there surviving evidence of this syncretism?
- How many sultans performed the Hajj, and can a systematic list be compiled with political consequences noted?
- Were there any post-1153 attempts to restore Buddhism or pre-Islamic practices? How were they suppressed?
- What is the relationship between the multiple Abu al-Barakat origin narratives (Moroccan, Somali, Persian), and can they be reconciled or used to identify different waves of Islamic influence?
