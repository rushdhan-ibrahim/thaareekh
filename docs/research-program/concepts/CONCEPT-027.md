# Concept Entry

Concept ID: `CONCEPT-027`
Last updated: `2026-02-09`
Category: `office/institution`

## 1) Canonical label
- Primary label: Shahbandar (port master)
- Alternate labels/spellings: *Shahbandar* (ޝަހެބަންދަރު); *Shabunder* (English variant); harbour master; port master; admiral-in-chief.
- Language/script forms: Persian *shah-bandar* (شاه بندر, "king of the port"); Dhivehi *Shahbandar*; Malay *syahbandar*; English "port master", "harbour master".

## 2) Definition
- Short definition: The port/maritime administration office in the Maldivian sultanate, combining trade regulation, customs collection, naval command, and international trade network management. A key institution reflecting the Maldives' position as an Indian Ocean maritime crossroads.
- Historical scope and periodization:
  - **Origin**: The *shahbandar* is a Persian-origin administrative title found across the Indian Ocean world, from the Persian Gulf to Southeast Asia (Malay sultanates). In the Maldives, it was adopted as part of the Islamic administrative vocabulary after 1153.
  - **Pyrard account (c. 1602-1607)**: The Shahbandar is listed among the cabinet ministers with the Navy portfolio, also described as admiral-in-chief. This dual trade/military function reflects the Maldives' dependence on maritime security for trade.
  - **Trade context**: The Maldives was a critical node in Indian Ocean trade networks, exporting cowrie shells (currency across the Indian Ocean), coir rope, dried fish, and ambergris, while importing rice, textiles, and metals. The Shahbandar regulated these flows.
  - **Revenue function**: Customs duties collected by the Shahbandar were a major source of state revenue, making this office economically powerful.
  - **Late monarchy**: The office evolved as trade patterns changed with European colonial influence (Portuguese, Dutch, British) and the shift from traditional Indian Ocean trade to colonial-era commerce.
- Why it matters in this genealogy graph: The Shahbandar's economic and naval power made the office politically significant. Shahbandar holders may appear in the graph as elite nodes connected to the royal family through marriage or service.

## 3) Semantic and historical notes
- The Shahbandar combined functions that modern states separate: customs/trade regulation, naval command, and port administration. This concentration of maritime authority in one office reflects the Maldives' archipelagic nature.
- The Shahbandar's naval function overlaps with the Dhoshimeynaa's military function (CONCEPT-020). The distinction may be between maritime military (Shahbandar) and land/garrison military (Dhoshimeynaa), though this requires further research.
- The cowrie shell trade was the Maldives' most important economic activity for centuries. The Shahbandar would have overseen this trade, connecting the Maldives to Bengal, East Africa, and Southeast Asia. See CONCEPT-024 for numismatic context.
- The Portuguese disruption of Indian Ocean trade (16th century) fundamentally challenged the Shahbandar's function and the Maldives' maritime economy.
- The *shahbandar* institution is found across the Indian Ocean Islamic world (Malacca, Gujarat, Hormuz, etc.), providing comparative context for the Maldivian version.
- Cross-reference with CONCEPT-002 (Furadaana) for the Shahbandar's position within the ministerial cabinet.
